Appendix 7

De‑identification techniques and privacy evaluation

Practical guidance for reducing re‑identification risk and selecting privacy evaluation methods for synthetic health data.

Use in Step 4 Privacy metrics Recommendations Open original PDF Download PDF

What de‑identification does

Break the link between a dataset and a real person so disclosed facts can’t be reasonably linked back to an identified individual.

Why privacy evaluation matters

Synthetic data can still leak membership or attributes when models overfit or preserve rare patterns; evaluation validates residual risk.

Who this is for

Data custodians, requestors, scientists, and governance reviewers documenting a re‑identification risk assessment.

De‑identification techniques

De‑identification is a risk management exercise: stronger privacy protection generally reduces data utility. Effectiveness depends on data type, context, and plausible re‑identification threats.

Aggregation

Reduce granularity by summarising values (e.g., counts, bands, regional roll-ups).

Suppression

Remove direct identifiers or high-risk values/records when they cannot be safely generalised.

Generalisation

Replace precise values with coarser categories (e.g., date of birth → age band).

Pseudonymisation

Transform identifiers (SLKs, hashing, encryption) while recognising residual linkage risk.

Perturbation

Introduce controlled noise or swapping/micro-aggregation to reduce singling-out.

Robustness checks

Test for both directions of re‑identification risk: matching a person to data and matching data to a person. Document assumptions about attacker knowledge and auxiliary data.

Privacy evaluation in synthetic data

No single score defines “privacy safe”. Use multiple metrics aligned to the threat model and interpret results in context. This table summarises commonly used families of privacy evaluation methods.

Threat-model reminders

  • Define attacker knowledge and access (e.g., quasi-identifiers, auxiliary datasets).
  • Separate membership, identity, and attribute disclosure risks.
  • Report baselines and decision thresholds used for governance sign-off.

Differential privacy (DP)

DP provides guarantees about the synthesis process, not a blanket guarantee about every dataset. If DP is used, document the privacy budget (ε) and validate empirical privacy risk with tests.

Consensus-based recommendations

Practical recommendations for reporting and decision-making during re‑identification risk assessments.

1

Base evaluations on quasi-identifiers (QIs).

2

Evaluate all records (not just samples).

3

Avoid stand‑alone similarity metrics for privacy claims.

4

Align membership disclosure tests to an explicit threat model.

5

Report prevalence‑adjusted scores and naive baselines.

6

Limit attribute disclosure claims to members when applicable.

7

Use non‑member baselines for comparison.

8

Apply dual thresholds (absolute + relative) for decisions.

9

Validate differential privacy empirically where used.

10

Report stochastic variation across multiple synthetic datasets.

Using this appendix in practice

Membership disclosure evaluation

  • Define threat model and attacker access.
  • Compute a naive baseline (random guessing / prevalence).
  • Report F1 (or equivalent) against baseline and thresholds.

Attribute disclosure evaluation

  • Select quasi-identifiers that reflect realistic auxiliary information.
  • Use non-member baselines when measuring disclosure risk.
  • Apply dual thresholds (absolute + relative) for decisions.
